Master’s Thesis – Rapid and Accurate Detection of E. coli O157:H7 Using LAMP

This thesis focused on developing a rapid, sensitive, and cost-effective molecular assay for detecting E. coli O157:H7 using colorimetric Loop-mediated Isothermal Amplification (LAMP). The work resulted in an optimized diagnostic method with strong specificity, visual detection capability, and a short turnaround time suitable for low-resource laboratories.

Abstract

E. coli O157:H7 is one of the major foodborne pathogens causing hemorrhagic colitis and hemolytic-uremic syndrome globally. In this project, the virulence gene eae was selected as the molecular marker, and specific LAMP primers were designed. Using both traditional optimization and Taguchi experimental design, key reaction parameters—including temperature, MgSO₄ concentration, and incubation time—were optimized. The final assay enabled clear visual detection within 40 minutes with high specificity and a detection limit of 3.2×10⁴ CFU/mL (0.38 fg).

Objectives

  • Develop a rapid, equipment-free detection method for E. coli O157:H7.
  • Design specific LAMP primers targeting the conserved eae virulence gene.
  • Optimize reaction parameters using Taguchi statistical design.
  • Evaluate assay sensitivity and specificity against common foodborne pathogens.

Methods

  • Primer design using PrimerExplorer v5
  • DNA extraction and 16S rRNA verification
  • LAMP optimization (MgSO₄, temperature, incubation time)
  • Colorimetric detection using SYBR Green and KBC Power Load dyes
  • Visualization via UV fluorescence and 2% agarose gel electrophoresis
  • Specificity testing using Salmonella typhi, V. cholerae, and P. aeruginosa

Results

The optimized LAMP assay successfully amplified the eae gene at 63°C within 40 minutes. Positive reactions generated bright green or pink fluorescence depending on the dye used, while negative controls showed no amplification. Sensitivity testing confirmed a detection limit of 0.38 fg. Specificity analysis demonstrated no cross-reaction with other pathogens.

My Role

I performed wet-lab experiments including DNA extraction, reaction setup, and agarose gel electrophoresis. I contributed to primer design, optimization of LAMP conditions, analysis of fluorescence results, preparation of figures, and scientific writing and data presentation for both the thesis and the related publication.
